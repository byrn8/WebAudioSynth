Frequency Envelope
The Frequency Envelope is the other envelope that is responsible for the alteration of the filter this time instead of the oscillator. The Frequency Envelope is composed of 4 main parts: Attack, Decay, Sustain, and Release. Each of these elements are responsible for different things and they will be explained in great detail in the coming subsections. The image below shows what the Frequency Envelope looks like on the synthesizer. As the fundamentals of both the Amplitude Envelope and the Frequency Envelope are the same, most of the information on this page will be the same as the Amplitude Envelope page so if you have read that page it might be best to skip this one.
Attack
The attack value is measured from a range of 0 to 2 and is responsible for determining the time in which the sine wave reaches it's maximum point (or volume to put it in simpler terms). In ADSR envelopes, the attack is the first value that is calculated and applied to the sine wave. The diagram below should give a better understanding of what the attack is responsible for in sound.
Image Source: Tone.js
Having a low attack value means that the sound that is produced will reach peak volume releatively quickly meaning that it will sound more 'immediate' to the ear. Having a high attack value means the complete opposite will occur and the sound will sound less 'immediate' and will gradually fade in and grow in volume until it reaches it's peak point later on. Playing around with the attack for any period of time should give most people a pretty good idea of how it can affect the final output of the sound as the difference between the low and high values are quite drastic. With that being said, the attack is one of the easier envelope elements to understand and ones such as decay can be a lot harder to grasp when just playing around with the synthesizer.
Decay
The decay value is also measured from a range of 0 to 2 and is responsible for determining the time between the end of the attack and the beginning of the sustain. In other words it is used as a kind of 'dropoff' value that calculates how fast the sine wave will drop in frequency. The decay is the second value of the ADSR ennvelope as you may have already guessed. The diagram below should give a better understanding of what the attack is responsible for in sound.
Image Source: Tone.js
When the decay is set to a low value on the synthesizer the sound that it produced will suddenly 'dropoff' drastically right after the attack has reached it's peak and will produce a kind of audibile 'clipping' sound that is made because the time between the attack and sustain is so low. When the value is set high the transition between the attack and the sustain will be much smoother as the time will be increased. To get the best understanding of what the decay value does audibly, set both of the attack values to 1.0 and the play around with the decay values as when the attack is set too low the decay can be quite hard to hear even when the values go from 0 to 2.
Sustain
The sustain value is measured from a range of 0 to 1 and is responsible for determining the time at which the sound of the sine wave 'rests' or 'sustains'. The sustain is used when you want to specify how long the mid point of the sine wave or sound to carry on for after the end of the decay period. The sustain is the third value of the ADSR envelope that is calculated right after the attack and decay. The diagram below should give a better understanding of what the attack is responsible for in sound.
Image Source: Tone.js
While it is pretty self explanatory, the lower you put the sustain value, the shorter the time of the midpoint of the sine wave will be and the higher you put the value the longer the midpoint will last. Getting a feel for how the sustain affects the sound is fairly simple as regardless pf the other 3 values, the sustain will always have an audible impact on the sound.
Release
The release value is measured from a range of 0 to 5 and is responsible for determining the time between the end of the sustain to the lowest point of the sine wave (which is also the point at which the sound or sine wave ends). The release is the final value of the ADSR envelope and is happens right after the sustain to the end of the sound. The diagram below should give a better understanding of what the attack is responsible for in sound.
Image Source: Tone.js
Fundamentaly, the release value acts in the exact same way as the decay value does except that instead of happening between the attack and sustain it happens between the sustain and end of the sine wave. The lower the release value, the shorter the time is between the end of the sustain and the end of the sound and the higher the value the longer the time will be. The 'clipping' that was mentioned in the decay section also happens to the release value as well if it is set too low. The release is also very easy to get a grasp of when trying to hear the audibile differences as it happens right at the end of the sounds lifecycle so just playing with the values will give you an idea of how the release value works.
Curves
The final element that makes up both of the envelopes is the curves for the attack, decay, and release values. The curves are the dropdown menus at the top of each of the respective elements that changes the way they act. Most of the curve types are fairly self explanatory but to give a bot of context, the curves essentially affect the way the elements get from point A to point B. If you're familiar with the terms 'linear' and 'exponential' then i'm sure you will gain understanding just from messing around with the different options as most of them are quite distinct from one another (especially the attack curves).
